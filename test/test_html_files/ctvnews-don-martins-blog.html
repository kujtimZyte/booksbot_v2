Don Martin | Blog

  • Don Martin: Here's an inconvenient truth, Trudeau's brand is broken

    We now have two different truths on the SNC-Lavalin scandal: one altruistic and one inconvenient, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: Two truths ahead as the SNC-Lavalin controversy heads for a showdown

    So the only question now: Was that pressure improper? If there is a definitive answer to that, it is the line of demarcation between this story as Trudeau government kryptonite or a fizzle that'll be forgotten by election day, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Keeping quiet means cover-up is the only conclusion

    Heres proof that a week can be a lifetime in politics, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Until the byelection is over, no NDP news is good news

    If you believe theres no such thing as bad news, the NDP had a banner day earlier this week, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Forget sunny ways, here comes the mud

    The spectre of voter manipulation from foreign operatives is real. But the greater threat to clear-sighted voting comes from the antagonists slinging made-up mud at each other under glass in the House of Commons, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: The blockade that killed pipeline investment

    This was the week international investment in Canadian energy transportation went palliative following multiple bouts of protracted suffering. The industry obit will detail how a few chiefs, bestowed with the ceremonial title by their ancestors, finally proved theres no way to move oil, bitumen or natural gas from the ground to the ocean, says Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Kevin Vickers would be a welcome change to political scene

    In a Canadian political realm hardening into partisan divides dominated by promise-breaking, polling-directed and scripted-to-the-comma political leaders, a welcome change is musing about entering a leadership race. A successful bid would be a big gain for New Brunswick and even bigger win for Canadian politics, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin's 2019 predictions: Another Liberal majority, and Singh steps down

    The oncoming year is going to be an epic one for Canadian politics with a general election, a key provincial election, a pivotal byelection and plenty of economic jitters here and around the world. Heres how 2019 could roll out.

  • Don Martin's 5 political power players of 2018

    Here are Don Martins picks for the top five political power players of the year:

  • Don Martin's story of the year: The plight of Alberta

    The imminent rollout of Canadian news stories of the year will be predictable. All important. All nation-defining. All second rate considerations to the main news event. For sustained national impact, intense political fallout and no clear route to resolution or solution on the horizon, theres only one most-newsworthy story, the plight of Alberta, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Pre-election grandstanding behind federal francophone huddle

    Consider the parliamentary emergency which rushed a handful of federal political party leaders, including the prime minister, into a huddle of five-alarm concern this week.

  • Don Martin: A deficit of logic as the government goes squirrelly on spending

    Finance Minister Bill Morneaus fiscal update has been welcomed by already-profitable businesses, although it spends deficit dollars in heady economic times, says Power Play host Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Taxpayers have spoken, winter games not worth the Olympic-sized bill

    For the first time in bidding-war history, a city which has already felt the magic of Olympic hosting gave the Games a pass. What should concern the International Olympic Committee even more than general global apathy for hosting rights was having a decisive Games rejection come from, of all places, Calgary, says Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Craig Oliver turns 80 credibility spanning decades

    Craig Oliver is usually among the first to show up in the Ottawa bureau in the morning.

  • Don Martin: The GG retirement package reeks of entitlement and extravagance

    All it takes to do the job is the stamina to smile through migraine-inducing diplomatic dinners, the odd bill-signing ceremony and the ability to drone out a Throne Speech every few years. But it turns out that the real dream is life after you leave Rideau Hall, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Nasty Canadian campaign coming? Look south to see the real thing

    Canada, brace yourself. Both the prime minister and the Official Opposition leader warn this year-long fight for your election vote is going to be particularly 'nasty.'

  • Don Martin: Trust me, there's no pardon on pot in American eyes

    Amid euphoric media coverage of the first Canadian to buy legal pot, the more interesting and ignored angle would be the last person charged with possession of cannabis in this country.

  • Don Martin: Michaelle Jean has lost the aura of someone worth defending

    Incredibly bright and charismatic with an instant connection to average Canadians, Michaelle Jean checked every box on a list to shake up the staid image of a Canadian Governor General. What happened to that Michaelle Jean is now a major international mystery, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Both sides are wrong turning McClintic transfer into parliamentary circus

    It's not often both major federal parties are right and wrong at the same time. But as the rehashing of the tragic 2009 murder of eight-year-old Tori Stafford echoes through the House of Commons, the Liberals and the Conservatives have accomplished this rare feat, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Canada at risk of becoming garbage pickers of the world

    With reports of Canada negotiating for a used surveillance drone, one stripped of its hi-tech navigating equipment and considered scrap-worthy by German defence officials, we are at risk of becoming the garbage pickers of the world, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Alleslev defection an argument for banning floor crossing

    There's a very simply solution that would save voters from being jerked around by MPs who see the Commons aisle as a red carpet welcome to personal gain and political revenge: ban it, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Jagmeet Singh's Burnaby byelection run takes more guts than brains

    Even among the truest believers in the NDP, doubts are growing about their rookie leader. They say Jagmeet Singh lacks communications skills beyond photo-ops, stands for everything left and nothing definitive at the same time and rarely projects the aura of being in command of his caucus.

  • Don Martin: Real or hypothetical, Alberta separation angst bound to grow

    Alberta separation talk is not intended as a threat. Yet. It's the sort of grumble which rumbles when Albertans feel they're undervalued as a mere gas tank for Canada's economic engine.

  • Don Martin: Scheer hopes ignoring Bernier will send him to political oblivion

    Conservative Leader Andrew Scheers response to renegade MP Maxime Bernier and his policy complaints is clearly to ignore him, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: Mad Max's backstabbing exit could unify Tories

    Maxime Berniers exit has the potential to unify the Conservatives in the same way as that partys runner-up leadership candidate Belinda Stronachs defection to the Liberals in 2005, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: Why bigger isn't always better in building a new cabinet

    It's bigger, but thats not to say it is any better. Boosted by five new ministerswho in turn will be bolstered by plenty of support staff and the costly perks of executive officethe Trudeau election-ready cabinet is bordering on bloated with overlaps and carved-out creations aimed at tackling nagging problems, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: MPs mulling their political best-before dates

    It's that time of the pre-election cycle when MPs ponder their private lives and then take the pulse of their ridings to gauge the odds of being rewarded with re-election or facing the risk of a voter pink slip.

  • Don Martin: As a boycott, U.S. ambassador's July 4th party was a bust

    While the U.S. ambassador's July 4th bash usually a social highlight of the summer in Ottawa was undeniably smaller than the 4,000 overcapacity crowd of recent years, but as a boycott, it was a bust, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Five ways the political landscape shifted this season

    After 195 shows covering 118 sitting days in the House of Commons, raging trade wars, travel controversies, ethical investigations and a lot of Donald Trump huffing and puffing, Power Play rests until September starting today. It's been a season of seismic shifts on multiple political landscapes, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Trump's America an embarrassment to this dual citizen

    I've always been proud of being born in the U.S.A., even after five decades as a Canadian citizen. Having access to dual citizenship in a country that gave so much to the world economically, militarily and democratically was a bonus branch from our family tree. Not any more, says Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Time for Trudeau to take aim at Trump's base

    This unprecedented White House hostility is a moment to take advantage of the confusion in an American public which doesnt understand why North Korea is lionized and Canada demonized by President Trump. The answer is not just retaliation, its also education, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Ruth Ellen Brosseau's political rise a welcome triumph

    Don Martin: The political rise of Ruth Ellen Brosseau to NDP House leader is a welcome triumph on Parliament Hill.

  • Don Martin: Parliamentarians should heed cautionary tales of Alexander, Leitch

    With one simple pledge not to seek re-election, Parliaments two most infamous star-crossed MPs -- Kellie Leitch and Chris Alexander -- have finally completed a double-helix dive into obscurity.

  • Don Martin: Is Trudeau playing politics with pro-choice funding stance?

    There's something queasy in forcing organizations applying for Canada Summer Job funding or support from the new Canada Service Corps. to attest that anti-abortion activity is not a core principle or mandate, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Political correctness behind Sir John A. pub name change

    The Sir John Public House in the Kingston law offices where Macdonald began mapping out a country called Canada, stripped the pub of his name on Tuesday.Surely this is a sign of hyper-reactive cleansing for the sake of political correctness, a small step toward the sort of Stalinist purging of people from Soviet history, says Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Trudeau showed questionable judgment in meeting with Boyle

    Back on December 18, the itinerary listed nothing about meeting with freed hostage Joshua Boyle and his family, whose five-year ordeal while held captive by Taliban-linked hostage-takers in Afghanistan ended in October. It was a curious omission, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin's political predictions for 2018

    After consulting insiders and veteran observers, Don Martin offers a glimpse the year ahead, and hopes his mostly-wrong 2017 predictions were a particularly miserable anomaly.

  • Don Martin: Surely Kent Hehr has talked himself out of cabinet

    At best Hehr's comments, which he doesn't totally deny saying, betray a tin political ear. At worst it projects a stony-hearted attitude which renders him unfit to be the minister in charge of a portfolio where empathy for the suffering is a leading prerequisite, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Bill Morneau's political fate in Mary Dawson's hands

    If you passed her on the street, you'd be reminded of a Mrs. Doubtfire or a friendly visitor to Mr. Rogers' Neighborhood. But if those gentle bespectacled eyes narrow and that unassuming tone turns inquisitional, the most powerful politicians in the land ought to be wary of Mary Dawson, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Parliament Hill skating rink a teeth-grinding waste of tax dollars

    The $5.6-million ice rink on Parliament Hill, which was extended from 24 to 83 days of operation this afternoon as public backlash started to grow is pointless icing on a bland birthday cake, says Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Retired cops chasing pot of gold at the end of legalization rainbow

    It's been four decades since two undercover cops ripped open the doors of my parked Rambler and busted all four of us for pot possession because they found some cannabis seeds in the ash tray.

  • Don Martin: Faced with political pushback Liberals developing a cut-and-run mentality

    Watching the Trudeau government dodge, weave and retreat through the prolonged crisis on taxation and ethics this fall, one question comes to mind: Are they smart or are they weak? asks Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Trudeau government needs to start sweating the small stuff

    It usually takes a full term or two for a government to achieve a level of sufficiently arrogant entitlement to boldly spew patronage, wildly squander money or rule by secretive decree - and it usually hits just before voters throw the bums out. But there are disquieting signs an early outbreak of second-term-itis is already infecting the sunny ways Trudeau government, writes Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Fixing Phoenix may be all but impossible

    It always seemed so inside baseball, a big problem for the public service but not a pressing concern in the real world, but the federal governments severely boondoggled Phoenix payroll system has now reached the level of implementation disaster thats cause for national taxpayer alarm, says Don Martin.

  • Don Martin: Despite damage control, Morneaus reputation has taken a hit

    When crisis communicators of the future need an example of a reputation-shredding failure to enact timely damage control, they will point to the Bill Morneau debacle. By pledging today to sell his Morneau Shepell shares and put all other assets in a blind trust, a minister who had a Boy Scout reputation lost the ability to claim a principled high ground response and was forced into surrender at knife point, Don Martin says.

  • Don Martin: Feds fumble on employee discounts a display of supersized political stupidity

    The updated edict from the Canada Revenue Agency clearly indicated that, while this is an extreme example, discounts and freebies were taxable benefits and must be declared as such on company and individual tax forms. In other words, the government unleashed a Big Mac tax attack, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: Time for Trudeau to quit the Harper blame game

    Theres a statute of limitations on political blame and the times up for this Liberal government, Don Martin says.

  • Don Martin: Morneau might be a one-term political wonder

    Staff confide Bill Morneau avoids the obligatory MP glad-handing in his Toronto riding. Maybe its understandable. This extremely wealthy businessman didnt enter politics to deliver small talk in church basement dinners. But the Finance Ministers apathetic attention to his Toronto Centre turf is noticeable enough for inside whispers to suggest he might not seek re-election two years from now.

  • Don Martin: Stephen Harpers political children poised for provincial takeovers

    Heres some interesting political trivia: No provincial premier has ever become prime minister of Canada. Thats what makes the phenomenon of Stephen Harpers political children so bizarre, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: Liberals smart to push ahead with closing the tax loopholes

    Its time for the T4 taxpayers of the nation to rise up. With professionals rolling out sob stories of proposed changes cramping their ability to hide taxes through incorporation, the most influential group remains silent on the sidelines, Don Martin says.

  • Don Martin: Trudeaus tepid reaction to Suu Kyis behaviour is astounding

    Over 7,000 Order of Canada decorations have been pinned on mostly-worthy recipients in the last 50 years. Over 90 Victoria Crosses have been given to Canadian soldiers for their supreme sacrifice. And 18 Stars of Military Valor have been awarded by Governors General for extreme bravery in battle.

  • Don Martin: Canada's 150th birthday is a weekend for celebration

    From gay rights to health care to ketchup chips, our nation should celebrate this Canada Day weekend, Don Martin says.

  • Don Martin's best and worst performers of the Trudeau cabinet

    Prime Minister Justin Trudeau will be releasing progress reports on his cabinet ministers, leading CTV's Power Play host Don Martin to give some of his own mid-term marks. Find out at CTVNews.ca who passes with flying colours and who needs a major improvement.

  • Don Martin: Appointments paralysis shows internal dysfunction

    The government's inability to fill judicial benches and top civil service jobs -- or pay public servants -- shows it's failing to get the basics done.

  • Don Martin: MPs avoid questioning foreign policy thats sure to be a winner with voters

    The opposition largely avoided questions about the Liberal government's new foreign and defence policies because they know Prime Minister Justin Trudeau and his cabinet hit a grand slam with them, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: On the great pipeline divide, PM Trudeau must pick a side

    The Great Divide doesn't just refer to the towering mountain range between British Columbia and Alberta -- it's also an apt description for the split over pipelines. Prime Minister Justin Trudeau is going to have to side with the economy, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: Will next CPC leader be a Liberal reign-ending nightmare?

    It's easy to ridicule Conservative fortunes as this baker's dozen of leadership hopefuls prepare to sell themselves as Prime Minister Justin Trudeau's worst political nightmare.

  • Don Martin: Trudeau government stacking up Alberta snubs

    From moving the National Energy Board to putting the infrastructure bank in Toronto, Prime Minister Justin Trudeau's government is finding a range of ways to snub Alberta, Don Martin says.

  • Don Martin: Opposition shadows cloud PM Trudeau's question period

    The move to have Prime Minister Justin Trudeau handle all the questions once a week is only going to make him hate the ritual more than he already does, Don Martin observes.

  • Don Martin: Let's hope common sense prevails on cross-border booze

    Almost within sight of Parliament Hill on the Quebec side of the river, theres a slightly-shabby but always-busy store where a law now headed for the Supreme Court is broken dozens of times every day.

  • Don Martin: Ontario takes politically smart, economically necessary action on housing

    You have to experience a bust to fear the bubble. Thats why Ontario is taking politically smart and economically necessary steps to open a small leak in what many experts call a hyper-inflated housing bubble in the Toronto area.

  • Don Martin: Real reefer madness was to think pot could be successfully outlawed

    Marijuana, outlawed in 1923 without parliamentary debate or any serious public pressure, will become legal to grow, buy and consume in 2018 under todays federal legislation. But theres a catch, which smacks of Liberal campaign promise remorse.

  • Don Martin: Senate doesn't have the constitution to oust Don Meredith

    Conventional wisdom has it that Senator Don Meredith will soon be tossed out the Senate door with a good riddance sticker on his back. But constitutional reality suggests the forced Meredith eviction will never happen.

  • Don Martin: Pot penalties ahead of legalization could have long-term impact

    Too many young people could face life-long trouble at the U.S. border if they're caught with marijuana and charged with possession in the remaining months before Canada decriminalizes the drug, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: Liberals try to rush rule changes to benefit themselves

    Opposition MPs are seething over an attempt by Prime Minister Justin Trudeau's government to change the rules of the House of Commons. That the Liberals are acting unilaterally smells like self-interest, Don Martin says.

  • Don Martin: Mexicans deported from U.S. planning to come to Canada

    About 4,000 kilometres southwest of Ottawa, migrant shelters line the Mexico border where, Reuters reports, many of those now being deported from Trumps America are plotting a Canadian invasion.

  • Don Martin: Parks Canada's bizarre political correctness blocks movie in Banff

    Parks Canada recently denied permission for the movie Hard Powder to shoot in Banff National Park. The denial elevates political correctness to a bizarre abstract art form, Don Martin writes.

  • Don Martin: Kellie Leitch video a glimpse of politically tortured soul

    Conservative leadership candidate Kellie Leitch was once earnest and charitable. That Leitch wouldn't recognize the one now running to lead the party.

  • Don Martin: Anti-Islamophobia motion triggered warped overreaction

    When it comes to expressing the will of Parliament, an MP-launched motion is the lowest Commons denominator.

  • Don Martin: Rona Ambrose's vacation neutralizes attack on PM Trudeau

    The rules are clear. Rona Ambrose did nothing technically wrong in yachting around the Caribbean last month with a billionaire buddy. So why does she act like she's been caught playing golf in Florida with Donald Trump between Nordstrom shopping expeditions for Ivanka fashion wear?
