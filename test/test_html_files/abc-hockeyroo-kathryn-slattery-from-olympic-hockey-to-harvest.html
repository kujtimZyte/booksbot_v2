Olympic hockey player Kathryn Slattery has stepped aside from high performance sport and taken her place on the family farm.

ABC Great Southern: Ellie Honeybone

Driving a header harvester in loops around the wide open paddocks of her family's West Australian farm, Kathryn Slattery is a long way from the bright lights of Rio de Janeiro.

The 2016 Olympian is one of the best hockey players in Australia but recently decided to step away from international representation and focus on harvest at home.

She may yet return to the Hockeyroos, but for now, farming is in her blood and Ms Slattery is relishing the change in pace and time spent with her family.

Back to Bundeera

Ms Slattery grew up on a farm in the tiny town of South Stirling, at the base of the picturesque Stirling Ranges in southern WA.

Despite the isolation, Ms Slattery and her siblings relished their rural childhood.

"Growing up here was amazing," she said.

"We caused a bit of a ruckus for mum and dad, but we were able to experience some very unique things and see the world in a different way.

"It's given me a lot of skills moving forward."

Ms Slattery attended a primary school with just 32 other students and first picked up a hockey stick by accident when asked to fill in for her brother's team.

"It was a weird way to come into the sport but I love the skill and I love the speed of the game," she said.

"I really loved being part of a team, so they were a few key factors for me to continue playing.

"I realised I was reasonably handy at it and by the time I was about 13 or 14, I really started to focus on hockey."

Ms Slattery made the choice to leave the rural lifestyle behind, moving to Perth to chase her dream at 15.

She made her senior international debut for the Hockeyroos in 2014 and competed in every major international tournament in the two-year lead-up to the 2016 Olympic Games in Rio.

A decade after first moving away and the 25-year-old is back home on the family farm, Bundeera, ready to start a new chapter.

Helping out with harvest in South Stirling is a world away from Olympic selection but it is a refreshing change for Slattery.

ABC Great Southern: Ellie Honeybone

The highs and lows of sport

Travelling the world playing sport at the highest level is not always an easy journey.

Ms Slattery experienced success and failure during her Hockeyroos career.

She played at the World Cup in 2018 but missed out on selection to the Commonwealth Games.

"The highs and lows, from a personal point of view, have been fantastic," she said.

"The lows have been difficult, but I enjoy them in the sense that you can get something out of it.

"You learn a lot of things about being an athlete, what it takes, and also how hard you can push yourself."

Ms Slattery said she views her Olympic debut as the pinnacle of her hockey career.

The Australian women's team finished in sixth place, with Slattery scoring twice in her team's quarter final loss to New Zealand.

"For me that was something I worked so hard for, really put everything on the line for," she said.

"To be able to run out on the pitch that first time and become an Olympian was a fantastic feeling.

"My parents and family were in the crowd and to have their backing was definitely the highlight of my career."

It was after Rio that things began to change within the administration and coaching side of Hockey Australia, and Ms Slattery began to question her future in the sport.

Time for a change

As instability crept into the organisation, Ms Slattery began to feel frustrated with Hockey Australia and found it difficult to stay motivated.

Coaches walked away and the dynamic of the group changed.

"I felt that I wasn't getting the satisfaction out of the environment that I had previously," she said.

"To stay motivated is definitely difficult for any high-performance athlete but when you're facing some uncertainty, it makes it more difficult every day to get up and get what you want out of the program.

"There have been a number of people within the group that had similar feelings."

Ms Slattery, feeling confident in her achievements and performances at the highest level, chose to take an indefinite break.

"At this point I'm unsure exactly where I want to head with hockey," she said.

"I really love the sport but there are other things I really want to pursue for a period of time.

"I'd like to travel and potentially play overseas.

"I might find that I really miss it … I left the door open for a purpose and had good conversations about the potential to come back."

Harvest calling

On top of gruelling training schedules, recovery, and travelling to matches, Ms Slattery completed a degree in Agribusiness, which is proving valuable as harvest wraps up for the season.

"It's a great time of year to come back — harvest is really exciting," she said.

"There's lots of things going on and I love the challenge of learning new things every day and being outside working hands-on.

"I'm quite a driven person, so I like to feel like I can make a difference and work hard to achieve something."

While she will miss her friends and teammates within the hockey world, Ms Slattery is thrilled to be back in her childhood home.

"I've had a huge following of people in the district and that's something I feel very proud of," she said.

"I hope that I have inspired people to push themselves and try and achieve something that they want to."

When asked if harvest was a welcome change from the strict routine of a full-time athlete, Ms Slattery said she was enjoying being able to eat whatever she wanted.

"It is nice to wake up and not feel like you have to do anything in particular," she said.

"It's nice to have the change and not feel the guilt from not doing the running."
