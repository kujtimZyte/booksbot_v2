Ghost bus: the 'ScoMo Express' hits the runway rather than the road

By Michael Koziol
5 November 2018 7:30pm
  • Share on Facebook
  • Share on Twitter
  • Share on Whatsapp
  • Send via Email

View all comments

The self-styled "ScoMo Express" might be tearing up the Bruce Highway this week, but for the most part, Prime Minister Scott Morrison is touring Queensland by jet.

Pictures of the big bus emblazoned with the PM's face flooded his social media accounts on Monday, at the start of a four-day trek from the Gold Coast to Townsville.

However, Fairfax Media has confirmed Mr Morrison and his staff will travel for most of the 1500 kilometre journey aboard a Royal Australian Air Force VIP jet.

The ghost bus will be left with only its driver on board for several key legs, including the 400 kilometre-plus stretch from the Sunshine Coast to Gladstone.

Advertisement

While Mr Morrison's office stressed the cost of the ScoMo Express was paid out of Liberal Party coffers, taxpayers will foot the bill for the VIP plane and hotel costs.

All MPs are entitled to travel for legitimate work purposes on the public purse, though the PM's Queensland visit has been criticised by some observers for resembling an election campaign.

Even during actual elections, taxpayers cover the travel costs until the parties hold their official campaign "launches" - which is why the "launches" are typically held very late in the campaign.

About half a dozen staffers usually accompany the Prime Minister on a trip, including two spinners, a policy adviser, executive officer and security.

The overnight travelling allowance that can be claimed by senior staff varies depending on location. For Brisbane it is $358 a night, while in Mackay it is $293.

Queensland is a crucial battleground state in the general election. Of nine Coalition-held seats with a margin of less than 2 per cent, five are in the Sunshine State: Capricornia, Flynn, Forde, Petrie and Dickson, held by Home Affairs Minister Peter Dutton.

The government's poor showing at the Longman byelection, to Brisbane's north, saw the Coalition's primary vote fall by nearly 9 per cent.

"Queensland is well over-represented in terms of marginal seats," said electoral analyst Ben Raue, who runs The Tally Room blog.

"[Those] seats are crucial if the Liberals are going to have a chance of holding on to government.

"Right now in the polls they're a long way away from holding government but if they were to get closer, then these Queensland seats will really matter."

Liberal politicians played down concerns about the party's fortunes in the Sunshine State. Luke Howarth, who holds the marginal seat of Petrie, said Mr Morrison was hopeful of picking up new seats such as Lilley, where Labor's Wayne Swan is retiring.

"He's just up here looking around, saying g'day and listening to people," Mr Howarth said. "I'm certainly not worried, I'm just getting on with the job."

Long-serving Queensland senator Ian Macdonald said he never despaired about the party's fate in his home state, predicting a better outcome than many expect. He said Mr Morrison's interest in Queensland was not a sign of "panic" but one of pragmatism.

"Queensland wins and loses elections - for the last 30 years," he said. "He [the PM] could easily be at the Melbourne Cup this week but hes not, he's in Queensland, which I appreciate."

Mr Morrison began the tour visiting a strawberry farm to unveil changes to the working holiday visa allowing backpackers to work in a wider range of regional areas, and for longer. He also announced $112 million to extend the Gold Coast light rail from Broadbeach to Burleigh Heads.

Asked whether his Queensland tour amounted to a quasi election campaign, Mr Morrison replied: "This is me doing what I do. I'm out, I'm listening, I'm hearing and I'm doing. That's what I'm doing as a Prime Minister."

Labor leader Bill Shorten and his deputy Tanya Plibersek regularly travel around the country at taxpayers' expense, too. Ms Plibersek - who lives in Sydney - spent Monday in Perth visiting schools and attending P&C events.

In the most recent reporting period, between April and June, Ms Plibersek and her Labor colleague Anthony Albanese spent more on domestic flights than all other NSW MPs. Meanwhile, Mr Shorten and opposition health spokeswoman Catherine King outspent all other Victorian MPs on domestic flights.
